PART 1: INDIA — CORE TRADE LAWS & REGULATIONS
================================================================================

1.1 FOREIGN TRADE (DEVELOPMENT AND REGULATION) ACT, 1992 (FTDR ACT)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Supreme legislation governing foreign trade in India.
- Empowers the Central Government to formulate the Foreign Trade Policy (FTP).
- No export or import can be made except in accordance with this Act.
- Key Provisions:
  * Section 3: Power of Central Govt to make orders for prohibition/restriction.
  * Section 5: DGFT empowered to issue licenses, permits, and certificates.
  * Section 6: Prohibition on exports without IEC (Importer Exporter Code).
  * Section 11: Penalties for contravention — up to ₹10,000 or 5x the value.

1.2 CUSTOMS ACT, 1962
------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Primary law governing customs duties, import/export procedures, clearance of goods, and enforcement.
- Key Provisions:
  * Section 11: Power to prohibit import/export of goods.
  * Section 12: Levy of customs duty on imported/exported goods.
  * Section 17: Self-assessment of duty by the importer.
  * Section 28: Recovery of duties not levied or short-levied.
  * Section 46: Entry of goods on importation (Bill of Entry).
  * Section 50: Entry of goods for export (Shipping Bill).
  * Sections 111-113: Goods liable to confiscation.
  * Section 114: Penalties for contravention.
  * Section 135: Criminal liability — imprisonment up to 7 years.
- Penalties:
  * Civil: Confiscation of goods + fines up to the value of goods.
  * Criminal: If goods value > ₹1 crore → non-bailable offense.
  * If goods value ≤ ₹1 crore → bailable offense.

1.3 FOREIGN EXCHANGE MANAGEMENT ACT (FEMA), 1999
------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Regulates foreign exchange transactions related to imports/exports.
- Managed by the Reserve Bank of India (RBI).
- Key Requirements:
  * All trade payments must go through authorized dealers (banks).
  * Export proceeds must be realized within 9 months (extended to 15 months in some cases).
  * Import payments must match the invoice and customs documents.
  * Any violation can lead to penalties up to 3x the amount involved.
- FEMA Regulations 2026 (effective Oct 1, 2026):
  * Consolidated and streamlined foreign exchange framework.
  * Replaces older notifications for improved clarity.

1.4 FOREIGN TRADE POLICY (FTP) 2023
------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Current trade policy of India — shifts from incentive-based to facilitation-based.
- Four Pillars:
  1. Incentive to Remission (replacing MEIS with RoDTEP/RoSCTL).
  2. Export Promotion through Collaboration.
  3. Ease of Doing Business (automation, paperless systems).
  4. Emerging Areas (e-commerce exports, districts as export hubs).
- Key Schemes:
  * Advance Authorization: Duty-free import of inputs for export production.
  * EPCG (Export Promotion Capital Goods): Duty-free import of capital goods against export obligation.
  * RoDTEP (Remission of Duties and Taxes on Exported Products).
  * RoSCTL (Rebate of State and Central Taxes and Levies) for textiles.
  * Districts as Export Hubs (DEH): Promoting exports at district level.
  * One-Time Amnesty Scheme: Resolve pending export obligation defaults.

1.5 IMPORTER EXPORTER CODE (IEC)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Mandatory 10-digit identification number from DGFT.
- Required for any entity engaged in import or export.
- Linked to the entity's PAN (Permanent Account Number).
- Valid for lifetime but must be updated annually on the DGFT portal.
- Exemptions: Personal use imports, diplomatic bags, certain govt agencies.

1.6 ITC (HS) CLASSIFICATION — INDIA
------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Indian Trade Classification based on the Harmonized System.
- 8-digit code system used to classify all goods.
- Categories:
  * FREE: No license required for import/export.
  * RESTRICTED: Requires license/authorization from DGFT.
  * PROHIBITED: Not permitted for trade under any circumstance.
  * STE (State Trading Enterprises): Only through designated entities.
- Check: Use the DGFT website (dgft.gov.in) to verify item policy.

1.7 SCOMET ITEMS (SPECIAL CHEMICALS, ORGANISMS, MATERIALS, EQUIPMENT & TECHNOLOGIES)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Dual-use items regulated under the Foreign Trade Policy.
- 8 Categories:
  0. Nuclear Materials and Equipment
  1. Toxic Chemical Agents and related equipment
  2. Organisms, toxins, and related equipment
  3. Missile delivery systems and related equipment
  4. Dual-use equipment (nuclear, chemical, biological)
  5. Aerospace systems
  6. Munitions List
  7. Electronics
- Export requires specific SCOMET license from DGFT.

PART 2: COUNTRY-SPECIFIC TRADE LAWS
================================================================================

2.1 UNITED STATES
------------------------------------------------------------------------
A) Export Controls:
   - ITAR (International Traffic in Arms Regulations):
     * Governs defense articles on the US Munitions List (USML).
     * Managed by DDTC (Directorate of Defense Trade Controls).
     * Companies must register with DDTC.
     * License required for export of defense articles.
   - EAR (Export Administration Regulations):
     * Governs dual-use items on the Commerce Control List (CCL).
     * Managed by BIS (Bureau of Industry and Security).
     * Items identified by ECCN (Export Control Classification Number).
     * License requirement depends on: ECCN + destination + end-user + end-use.
   - OFAC Sanctions:
     * Administered by Treasury Department.
     * Economic sanctions against targeted countries, entities, individuals.
     * Restricted parties lists: Entity List, SDN List, Unverified List.

B) Import Regulations:
   - HTSUS (Harmonized Tariff Schedule of the United States):
     * 10-digit classification code for all imported goods.
     * Published by the US International Trade Commission (USITC).
     * Determines duty rates, statistical categories.
   - Key Agencies: CBP (Customs and Border Protection), FDA, USDA, EPA, CPSC.
   - Section 301 Tariffs: Special tariffs on goods from specific countries.
   - Section 232 Tariffs: National security tariffs (steel, aluminum).
   - Anti-Dumping and Countervailing Duties (AD/CVD).

C) Penalties:
   - ITAR: Fines up to $1 million per violation, imprisonment up to 20 years.
   - EAR: Fines up to $300,000 per violation or twice the transaction value.
   - Criminal: Fines up to $1 million, imprisonment up to 20 years.

2.2 EUROPEAN UNION
------------------------------------------------------------------------
A) CE Marking:
   - Mandatory for many product categories (electronics, machinery, toys, medical devices).
   - Indicates compliance with EU safety, health, and environmental requirements.
   - Manufacturer creates technical file + EU Declaration of Conformity.
   - Recent Updates: Battery Regulation (EU) 2023/1542, AI Act, General Product Safety Regulation.

B) REACH (Registration, Evaluation, Authorisation, Restriction of Chemicals):
   - Applies to all chemicals manufactured/imported into EU (≥1 tonne/year).
   - Registration with ECHA (European Chemicals Agency) mandatory.
   - Restricts Substances of Very High Concern (SVHC).
   - Authorisation List for restricted chemicals.
   - Periodic updates to restricted substance lists.

C) RoHS Directive (Restriction of Hazardous Substances):
   - Limits hazardous materials in electrical and electronic equipment (EEE).
   - 10 restricted substances: Lead, Mercury, Cadmium, Hexavalent Chromium, PBB, PBDE, DEHP, BBP, DBP, DIBP.
   - Self-certified via Declaration of Conformity.
   - Exemptions available for specific applications.

D) Customs Codes:
   - HS Code: 6-digit global code.
   - CN Code: 8-digit EU Combined Nomenclature.
   - TARIC Code: 10-digit EU integrated tariff code.
   - TARIC includes: duty rates, anti-dumping duties, import controls.
   - Reference: European Commission TARIC consultation website.

E) CBAM (Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism):
   - Transitional phase started Oct 2023.
   - Reporting requirements for importers of: iron/steel, cement, aluminum, fertilizers, electricity, hydrogen.
   - Full implementation with mandatory carbon certificates from 2026.

F) GDPR Compliance for Cross-Border Trade:
   - Data protection requirements for customer data in trade.
   - Affects e-commerce and digital trade operations.

2.3 CHINA
------------------------------------------------------------------------
A) Food Safety & GACC Registration:
   - All overseas food manufacturers must register with GACC.
   - Current regulation: Decree 248 (being replaced by Order No. 280, effective June 2026).
   - High-risk foods (meat, dairy, seafood, infant formula): Official recommendation required from exporting country's authority.
   - Low-risk foods (snacks, beverages): Self-registration via CIFER system.
   - Mandatory bilingual labels (Simplified Chinese required).

B) Cosmetics Regulation:
   - Regulated by NMPA under CSAR framework.
   - "Special cosmetics" (sunscreens, hair dyes): Registration required.
   - "General cosmetics": Filing notification required.
   - Electronic submissions only (since Sep 2024).
   - Rigorous safety assessments mandatory.
   - Non-animal testing methods increasingly accepted.

C) Electronics:
   - CCC Certification (China Compulsory Certification) mandatory for many electronics.
   - China RoHS: Restrictions on hazardous substances (aligned with GB/T 39560 standards since Mar 2024).
   - EMC standard GB 4343.1-2024: Full enforcement from June 2026.
   - Products must comply with GB (Guobiao) national standards.

D) Key Import Steps:
   - Verify classification and registration requirements.
   - Ensure Chinese-language labeling.
   - Maintain supply chain traceability.
   - Document: safety reports, certificates of origin, compliance certificates.

2.4 UNITED ARAB EMIRATES (UAE)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
A) Core Requirements:
   - Valid trade license from DED (Department of Economic Development) or Free Zone Authority.
   - Customs Code (Import/Export Code) mandatory for clearing shipments.
   - Standard 5% customs duty on CIF value.
   - 5% VAT generally applicable.

B) Documentation:
   - Commercial invoice, packing list, bill of lading/airway bill.
   - Certificate of origin.
   - Import permits for restricted goods.

C) Free Zones:
   - 0% customs duty for goods imported into free zones.
   - 100% foreign ownership allowed.
   - Selling from free zone to UAE mainland requires customs clearance.
   - Major free zones: JAFZA (Jebel Ali), DAFZA (Dubai Airport Free Zone), SAIF Zone, RAK FTZ.

D) Halal Certification:
   - MANDATORY for food products (especially meat/poultry), cosmetics, pharmaceuticals.
   - Governed by MoIAT (Ministry of Industry and Advanced Technology).
   - Certification through EIAC-accredited Halal Certification Bodies.
   - Requirements: Halal ingredients, contamination-free manufacturing, supply chain traceability.

E) Emirates Conformity Assessment Scheme (ECAS):
   - Certificate of Conformity required for regulated products.
   - Emirates Quality Mark (EQM) for quality and safety compliance.

F) Prohibited Items:
   - Narcotic substances, counterfeit goods, materials conflicting with Islamic teachings.
   - Restricted: Medicines, chemicals, weaponry (require ministry approval).

2.5 JAPAN
------------------------------------------------------------------------
A) Food Imports:
   - Governed by Food Sanitation Act.
   - Notification to MHLW quarantine station required at port of entry.
   - Laboratory inspections for chemicals, additives, contaminants, pathogenic microbes.
   - Positive List System: Only approved additives permitted; unapproved = rejected.
   - Strict Food Labeling Act (updated April 2026): allergens, storage, heating instructions.

B) Pharmaceutical and Medical Device Act (PMD Act):
   - Regulates pharmaceuticals, quasi-drugs, cosmetics, medical devices, regenerative medicine.
   - Marketing Authorization Holder (MAH) designation required for commercial import/sale.
   - Personal importation: Small quantities allowed (2-month supply for non-prescription).
   - Exceeding limits requires Import Confirmation (Yakkan Shoumei).

C) JIS (Japanese Industrial Standards):
   - National standards for industrial products (quality, safety, compatibility).
   - Primarily voluntary but often referenced in regulations.
   - Critical for government procurement.
   - JIS mark available via third-party certification.

D) Key Agencies:
   - MHLW: Food sanitation and pharmaceutical affairs.
   - METI: Industrial standards and trade.
   - MAFF: Agriculture, forestry, and fisheries.
   - Japan Customs: Import procedures and tariff information.

PART 3: PRODUCT-SPECIFIC REGULATIONS
================================================================================

3.1 FOOD & AGRICULTURAL PRODUCTS
------------------------------------------------------------------------
A) India:
   - FSSAI (Food Safety and Standards Authority of India) regulations.
   - FSSAI license mandatory for food importers.
   - Import allowed only through designated ports.
   - Products must comply with Indian food safety standards.
   - Labeling: MRP, ingredients, nutritional info, FSSAI logo, "best before" date required.
   - Specific restrictions: Genetically modified foods, certain pesticide residue levels.

B) US (FDA):
   - Prior Notice of imported food required via FDA.
   - FSMA (Food Safety Modernization Act): Preventive controls, FSVP (Foreign Supplier Verification Programs).
   - Labeling: Nutrition Facts, allergen declarations.
   - Banned: Kinder Surprise eggs, certain unpasteurized cheeses.

C) EU:
   - EU General Food Law Regulation (EC) 178/2002.
   - RASFF (Rapid Alert System for Food and Feed) for unsafe products.
   - Strict MRL (Maximum Residue Levels) for pesticides.
   - Protected designations: PDO, PGI, TSG for regional specialties.

D) China:
   - GACC registration mandatory (see Section 2.3).
   - GB national food safety standards.
   - Import Food Safety Management Measures.

3.2 PHARMACEUTICALS & MEDICAL DEVICES
------------------------------------------------------------------------
A) India:
   - CDSCO (Central Drugs Standard Control Organisation) regulates.
   - Drug Controller General of India (DCGI) approves imports.
   - Import license required under the Drugs & Cosmetics Act, 1940.
   - Registration certificate + test license needed.
   - Medical devices now classified in 4 classes (A, B, C, D) under Medical Device Rules, 2017.

B) US:
   - FDA approval required (NDA, ANDA, 510(k) for devices).
   - Good Manufacturing Practice (GMP/cGMP) compliance.
   - Drug Listing & Establishment Registration.
   - Medical devices classified into Class I, II, III.

C) EU:
   - Medical Device Regulation (EU MDR 2017/745).
   - In Vitro Diagnostic Regulation (IVDR 2017/746).
   - CE marking mandatory for medical devices.
   - Conformity assessed by Notified Bodies.

D) Japan:
   - PMD Act regulates all pharmaceuticals and medical devices.
   - MAH (Marketing Authorization Holder) required.
   - PMDA (Pharmaceuticals and Medical Devices Agency) approval.

3.3 TEXTILES & APPAREL
------------------------------------------------------------------------
A) India:
   - Quality Control Orders (QCOs) for certain textile items.
   - BIS certification may be required.
   - RoSCTL scheme for textile exporters (rebate of taxes).
   - Textile Committee registration for quality marks.

B) US:
   - Textile Fiber Products Identification Act: Fiber content labeling required.
   - Flammable Fabrics Act: Safety standards for clothing.
   - Country of Origin marking mandatory.
   - Berry Amendment: Certain textiles for military must be US-made.

C) EU:
   - Textile Regulation (EU) 1007/2011: Labeling requirements.
   - REACH compliance for chemicals used in textiles.
   - Azo dyes restriction (carcinogenic dyes banned).
   - CE marking not typically required for textiles.

3.4 ELECTRONICS & ELECTRICAL EQUIPMENT
------------------------------------------------------------------------
A) India:
   - BIS (Bureau of Indian Standards) certification mandatory for many electronics.
   - Compulsory Registration Scheme (CRS) under Electronics & IT Goods Order.
   - TEC (Telecommunication Engineering Centre) approval for telecom equipment.
   - E-Waste Management Rules, 2016: EPR (Extended Producer Responsibility).

B) US:
   - FCC (Federal Communications Commission) certification for electronic devices.
   - UL (Underwriters Laboratories) safety certification.
   - ENERGY STAR: Voluntary energy efficiency certification.
   - CPSC standards for consumer electronics.

C) EU:
   - CE marking mandatory.
   - EMC Directive (2014/30/EU): Electromagnetic compatibility.
   - LVD (Low Voltage Directive 2014/35/EU): Electrical safety.
   - RoHS + REACH compliance.
   - WEEE Directive: Waste electrical and electronic equipment recycling.

D) China:
   - CCC (China Compulsory Certification).
   - China RoHS compliance.
   - GB standards for safety and EMC.

3.5 CHEMICALS & HAZARDOUS MATERIALS
------------------------------------------------------------------------
A) India:
   - Manufacture, Storage and Import of Hazardous Chemical Rules, 1989.
   - DGFT restrictions on import of certain chemicals.
   - BIS standards for chemical products.
   - Ministry of Environment approval for hazardous chemicals.

B) US:
   - TSCA (Toxic Substances Control Act).
   - EPA registration for pesticides (FIFRA).
   - DOT regulations for transport of hazardous materials.
   - OSHA workplace chemical safety.

C) EU:
   - REACH Regulation (primary chemical regulation).
   - CLP Regulation (Classification, Labelling, Packaging).
   - Biocidal Products Regulation.
   - Prior Informed Consent (PIC) Regulation for restricted chemicals.

3.6 GEMS, JEWELRY & PRECIOUS METALS
------------------------------------------------------------------------
A) India:
   - GJEPC (Gem & Jewellery Export Promotion Council) registration.
   - Hallmarking Act 2000 (mandatory BIS hallmarking for gold jewelry).
   - Import of gold restricted to nominated agencies and refineries.
   - SEZ/EOU scheme for duty-free raw material import.

B) Kimberley Process:
   - International certification scheme for rough diamonds.
   - India is a participant; KP certificate required for import/export.
   - Prevents trade in conflict diamonds.

PART 4: HARMONIZED SYSTEM (HS) CODE CLASSIFICATION
================================================================================

4.1 OVERVIEW
------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Developed by the World Customs Organization (WCO).
- Used by 200+ countries, covering 98%+ of global merchandise trade.
- Updated every 5 years (latest: HS 2022).

4.2 STRUCTURE
------------------------------------------------------------------------
- 6-digit standardized code (global):
  * First 2 digits: CHAPTER (e.g., 09 = Coffee, Tea, Spices)
  * Next 2 digits: HEADING (e.g., 0902 = Tea)
  * Last 2 digits: SUBHEADING (e.g., 090210 = Green Tea)
- Countries add additional digits:
  * India: 8-digit ITC(HS) code
  * US: 10-digit HTSUS code
  * EU: 10-digit TARIC code

4.3 MAJOR HS CHAPTERS (EXAMPLES)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Chapters 01-05: Live Animals and Animal Products
- Chapters 06-14: Vegetable Products
- Chapter 15: Animal/Vegetable Fats and Oils
- Chapters 16-24: Prepared Foodstuffs, Beverages, Tobacco
- Chapter 25-27: Mineral Products (incl. Petroleum)
- Chapters 28-38: Chemical Products
- Chapters 39-40: Plastics and Rubber
- Chapters 41-43: Leather and Furskins
- Chapters 44-46: Wood and Wood Articles
- Chapters 47-49: Pulp, Paper, Printed Materials
- Chapters 50-63: Textiles and Textile Articles
- Chapters 64-67: Footwear, Headgear, Umbrellas
- Chapters 68-70: Stone, Ceramic, Glass
- Chapter 71: Gems, Precious Metals, Jewelry
- Chapters 72-83: Base Metals (Iron, Steel, Copper, Aluminum, etc.)
- Chapters 84-85: Machinery and Electrical Equipment
- Chapters 86-89: Vehicles, Aircraft, Vessels
- Chapter 90: Optical, Medical Instruments
- Chapter 91: Clocks and Watches
- Chapter 92: Musical Instruments
- Chapter 93: Arms and Ammunition
- Chapters 94-96: Furniture, Toys, Miscellaneous
- Chapter 97: Works of Art, Antiques

4.4 WHY ACCURATE CLASSIFICATION MATTERS
------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Determines applicable tariff rates and duties.
- Identifies regulatory requirements (licenses, safety standards).
- Affects eligibility for FTA preferential tariffs.
- Misclassification can lead to: financial penalties, customs delays, shipment seizure.
- Critical for trade statistics and policy analysis.

PART 5: FREE TRADE AGREEMENTS (FTAs) — INDIA
================================================================================

5.1 OVERVIEW
------------------------------------------------------------------------
India has signed comprehensive trade agreements with multiple countries/blocs:

A) CEPA (Comprehensive Economic Partnership Agreements):
   - India-Japan CEPA (in force since Aug 2011)
   - India-South Korea CEPA (in force since Jan 2010; upgrade ongoing)
   - India-UAE CEPA (in force since May 2022)
   - India-Oman CEPA (signed Dec 2025)
   - India-Australia ECTA (Economic Cooperation and Trade Agreement, in force since Dec 2022)

B) FTAs:
   - ASEAN-India FTA (AIFTA/AITIGA): Trade in goods effective Jan 2010.
   - India-Sri Lanka FTA (ISLFTA)
   - India-Thailand FTA (Early Harvest Scheme)
   - India-Singapore CECA
   - India-Malaysia CECA
   - India-Chile PTA (Preferential Trade Agreement)
   - India-MERCOSUR PTA
   - India-Afghanistan PTA

C) Regional Agreements:
   - SAFTA (South Asian Free Trade Area): Among SAARC nations.
   - APTA (Asia-Pacific Trade Agreement): India, Bangladesh, China, Laos, South Korea, Sri Lanka.

5.2 BENEFITS OF FTAs
------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Lower or zero customs duties on eligible goods.
- Expanded market access for textiles, pharma, engineering goods, gems & jewelry.
- Increased services trade (IT, professional services).
- Investor protection and dispute resolution mechanisms.
- Technology transfer and supply chain integration.

5.3 RULES OF ORIGIN (RoO)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Prevent trade deflection (non-originating goods claiming FTA benefits).
- Key Criteria:
  * Wholly Obtained: Goods fully produced/harvested in the exporting country.
  * Substantial Transformation: Non-originating materials undergo significant processing.
    - Regional Value Content (RVC): Min % of value added in FTA region (e.g., 35% under AIFTA).
    - Change in Tariff Classification (CTC/CTSH): HS code change during processing.
- Certificate of Origin (CoO) required to claim preferential tariffs.
- CAROTAR (2020): Indian regulation ensuring strict RoO compliance for imports.

PART 6: NOTABLE TRADE DISPUTE CASES
================================================================================

6.1 WTO DISPUTES INVOLVING INDIA
------------------------------------------------------------------------

CASE 1: Bangladesh v. India — Lead Acid Batteries (DS306)
- Issue: India imposed anti-dumping duties on Bangladesh's lead acid battery exports.
- Significance: One of the first cases where an LDC challenged a major economy at WTO.
- Outcome: Resolved through negotiation; India terminated anti-dumping duties in 2005.
- Lesson: WTO mechanism is accessible and effective even for developing nations.

CASE 2: India/Thailand v. US — Shrimp Dispute (DS343)
- Issue: US imposed anti-dumping duties on shrimp imports using "zeroing" methodology.
- Outcome: WTO ruled in favor of India/Thailand. US zeroing methodology found inconsistent with WTO Anti-Dumping Agreement.
- Impact: Significant reduction in anti-dumping duties on Indian shrimp.
- Lesson: Zeroing is generally prohibited under WTO rules.

CASE 3: EU v. India — Anti-dumping measures (DS304)
- Issue: EC challenged 27 anti-dumping measures imposed by India on steel and pharmaceutical goods.
- Allegation: Inconsistent with WTO Anti-Dumping Agreement.
- Outcome: Resolved through consultations; India reviewed and terminated many measures.

CASE 4: US v. India — Export Promotion Schemes (DS541)
- Issue: US challenged India's export subsidy programs (MEIS, SEZ benefits, etc.).
- Allegation: Violated WTO rules on prohibited subsidies (SCM Agreement).
- Outcome (2019): WTO panel ruled against India. Several export subsidy schemes found inconsistent.
- Impact: Led to major domestic policy adjustments (replacement of MEIS with RoDTEP).

CASE 5: India-US Mutual Resolution (2023)
- Issue: Six outstanding disputes terminated by mutual agreement.
- Scope: Steel countervailing duties, solar cells/modules, renewable energy, export incentives.
- Both nations removed retaliatory customs duties.

6.2 ANTI-DUMPING CASES — INDIA
------------------------------------------------------------------------

CASE 6: India's Anti-Dumping Investigation on Chinese Steel
- Products: Hot-rolled flat products of stainless steel, certain steel products.
- Outcome: India imposed anti-dumping duties ranging from $35-$558 per tonne.
- Authority: DGTR (Directorate General of Trade Remedies).
- Duration: Typically 5 years with sunset review provisions.

CASE 7: India v. China — Solar Cells and Modules
- Issue: Alleged dumping of solar cells from China at below-market prices.
- Investigation by DGTR.
- India imposed safeguard duty on solar cell imports (July 2018 — July 2020).
- Subsequently replaced with BCD (Basic Customs Duty) of 40% on modules and 25% on cells.

CASE 8: India — Anti-dumping on Caustic Soda from Multiple Countries
- Countries involved: China, Japan, South Korea, Qatar, Oman, Saudi Arabia, Taiwan, Thailand, USA.
- Product: Caustic soda (sodium hydroxide).
- Outcome: Various anti-dumping duties imposed based on individual investigation findings.

6.3 TRADE FRAUD & CUSTOMS ENFORCEMENT CASES — INDIA
------------------------------------------------------------------------

CASE 9: Customs Duty Fraud — Delhi Industrialist (₹190 Crore Case)
- Issue: Imported goods undervalued; export documents forged.
- Method: Duty-free imports diverted into domestic market.
- Amount: ₹190 crore involved.
- Agencies: DRI (Directorate of Revenue Intelligence), Customs.
- Penalties: Confiscation, fines, criminal prosecution.

CASE 10: Chennai Airport Smuggling Crackdown (2023)
- Seizure: ₹14 crore worth of contraband (gold, iPhones, laptops).
- Scale: 113 passengers intercepted.
- Outcome: 20 Customs officials transferred; investigation into negligence/complicity.
- Methods used by smugglers: Concealment in body cavities, electronic devices, food packaging.

CASE 11: Gold Smuggling Statistics (2023-24)
- DRI seized 1,319 kg of gold in FY 2023-24.
- Trend: Smugglers shifted to land borders and coastal routes from airports.
- Methods: Body concealment, modified vehicles, fishing boats.

CASE 12: Commercial Fraud and FTA Misuse (2023-24)
- 485 import cases booked for illegal benefits totaling ₹5,900+ crore.
- Methods: Mis-declaration, undervaluation, misuse of Free Trade Agreements.
- Focus: Trade-based money laundering and duty evasion.

CASE 13: Narcotics Seizure via Trade Routes (2023-24)
- DRI seized 107.31 kg of cocaine through air passenger routes.
- Trend: Increased cocaine smuggling through air cargo and couriers.

6.4 INTERNATIONAL TRADE DISPUTE CASES
------------------------------------------------------------------------

CASE 14: US-China Trade War (2018-ongoing)
- US imposed Section 301 tariffs on $370 billion of Chinese goods.
- China retaliated with tariffs on US agricultural/automotive products.
- Impact: Global supply chain disruptions, trade diversion to India, Vietnam, others.
- WTO: Multiple complaints filed by both sides.

CASE 15: Japan-Korea Export Controls Dispute (2019)
- Japan restricted exports of semiconductor materials to South Korea.
- Products: Fluorinated polyimide, photoresists, hydrogen fluoride.
- Issue: Historical/political tensions affecting trade.
- WTO: South Korea filed complaint; matter still under consultation.

CASE 16: EU Carbon Border Tax Disputes
- EU CBAM mechanism challenged by developing countries.
- Concern: Acts as disguised trade barrier, disadvantaging emerging economies.
- Impact: Affects steel, aluminum, cement exports from India, China, others.

PART 8: TRADE DOCUMENTATION CHECKLIST

8.1 STANDARD EXPORT DOCUMENTS (FROM INDIA)
